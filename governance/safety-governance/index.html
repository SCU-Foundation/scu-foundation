Safety Governance

Safety Governance defines the minimum safety requirements, auditability rules, and enforcement mechanisms that apply across the SCU architectural definition and the Lattice Protocol. It ensures that all certified agents, devices, and runtimes operate under verifiable safety guarantees.

Purpose of Safety Governance

Safety Governance ensures that the Lattice Protocol and the SCU architectural definition maintain strong safety guarantees across perceptual, behavioural, semantic, and domain layers. It defines the minimum requirements that all certified systems must meet.

This framework supports public sector adoption, institutional trust, and long term stability.

Governance Bodies Involved

Safety Governance operates across the three governance bodies defined in the Institutional Structure.

  • SCU Foundation. Defines safety requirements, auditability rules, and certification criteria.
  • Lattice Research Institute. Conducts semantic and interpretability research that informs safety requirements.
  • Post Silicon Collective. Publishes long horizon signals that may influence future safety considerations.

Safety Domains

Safety Governance covers four primary domains:

  • Perceptual Safety. Ensures that agents process inputs safely and predictably.
  • Behavioural Safety. Ensures that agent actions remain within safe and verifiable boundaries.
  • Semantic Safety. Ensures that meaning remains stable and non manipulative across interactions.
  • Domain Safety. Ensures that domain boundaries and separation guarantees are respected.

These domains align with the architecture defined in the Architecture Model.

Minimum Safety Requirements

All certified agents, devices, and runtimes must meet the following minimum safety requirements:

  • verifiable identity and provenance
  • attested execution environment
  • safe perceptual input handling
  • semantic stability and interpretability
  • domain separation compliance
  • capability negotiation through safe extension rules
  • auditability and traceability

These requirements are enforced through certification and attestation mechanisms defined by the SCU Foundation.

Certification and Enforcement

Safety Governance is enforced through certification. Certification ensures that systems comply with identity, safety, and semantic requirements before they participate in the ecosystem.

Certification includes:

  • agent certification
  • device certification
  • runtime certification
  • extension certification
  • attestation and provenance verification

Certification rules are published through the Lattice Protocol certification framework.

Integration with Change Management

Safety requirements influence how proposals are evaluated through the Change Management framework. Proposals must demonstrate that they maintain or improve safety guarantees.

This ensures that the protocol evolves without compromising safety.

Transparency and Auditability

Safety Governance requires that all safety related decisions, certification rules, and auditability mechanisms are published according to the Transparency framework.

This includes:

  • public certification criteria
  • auditability requirements
  • attestation rules
  • change logs for safety related updates
